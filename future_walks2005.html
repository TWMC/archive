Nikko

We would climb the extinct volcanoe Nantai-san (2484 m high, 8km, easy-medium, 1200 height meters, 4-6 hours). From the crater rim we will have stunning views down on the lake Chuuzenjii-ko, Senjoo-go-hara, and to the Oku-shirane-san mountain range. For the more serious hikers, and if the distribution of cars, drivers and people permits, we have the option to do the Senjoo-ga-hara hike afterwards, which is an easy 2-2.5 hrs, 9km hike across pittoresque marshland. We can then meet up with the more relaxed people at the impressive Yu-daki waterfalls, or at the Yumoto or other onsen. Difficulty ** to ***.

Iwaki waterfalls

The Iwaki Waterfall Walk is a beautiful and easy walk through a river gorge in Fukushima Prefecture. Along the path are about 10 to 12 waterfalls. There are also many clear pools below the falls for anyone interested in going for a swim. Following the walk, we will go to a Japanese restaurant. This walk is classified as easy to moderate. There are some fixed chains and ladders and a couple of steep (but not dangerous) slopes. As there are places where we will be walking on wet rocks, hiking boots are recommended but not required. Walking time is about 4 hours. Difficulty: **

Kita-dake (Minami Alps, Yamanashi Pref.)

TWMC is returning to Kita-dake in the Southern Alps after 5 years. Standing at 3192 metres, it is the second tallest mountain in Japan, and is counted among the Nihon Hyakumeizan, or Japan's 100 most famous mountains. The continuous slog uphill in the heat and the rarefied atmosphere is a great exercise for the heart and the lungs, but you will be rewarded with the breathtaking panoramic view from the summit (see our own hike report).  Difficulty ***.

Fuji-san (Shizuoka/Yamanashi-ken)

When you speak about Japan one of the first questions people ask you is, "Did you see Mount Fuji?". You can certainly answer "Yes", but when comes the next question, "Did you climb it?", what do you reply? As in other years, TWMC will offer you the opportunity to see Japan at its top, and to enjoy the sunrise there. Although Fuji-san is the highest mountain in Japan (3,776 m), climbing it is not very difficult due to its conical shape, but breathing can become more difficult near the summit due to the lower air pressure. The date of this hike may be shifted in case of bad weather. Difficulty: ***

Tateyama

The Tateyama Range forms the northwestern region of the Kita Alps, and includes some of the grandest peaks in Japan. Tateyama itself , one of the three great holy peaks, is conquerable by most, although those wishing for adventure could combine this walk with the nearby Tsurugi, regarded as the most difficult to climb of Fukada Kyuuya's 100 Famous Mountains. From Tateyama, I am considering one of Japan's classic long distance walks to Kamikochi over the peaks of Yakushi-dake, Mitsumata-renge-dake, and Yari-ga-take (Okuhotaka-dake and the notorious Daikiretto are also possible from Yari). If you are interested in this, please let me know as soon as possible. Difficulty: *** to *****

Yatsu-ga-take

According to ancient legend, Yatsu-ga-take was once higher than Fuji-san, but Fuji rose and kicked its rival over, forming eight lower peaks. There are actually more than eight peaks along the north-south ridge which forms the border between Nagano and Yamanashi-ken. This walk will allow three days to complete the trail over Yatsu-ga-take's highest peaks and most spectacular scenery. Difficulty: ****

Shirakami-sanchi (Akita/Aomori-ken)

The Shirakami Mountain Range, which runs along the border between Akita and Aomori Prefectures, and contains Japan's largest virgin beech forest, was registered as a "World Heritage Natural Resource" in 1993, along with the other natural heritage, Yakushima island. The Shirakami Mountains extend over 450 sq. km, and comprise a maze of steep sided hills with summits lying between 1,000-1,200m. Mountains in this region include Mt Mukai-shirakami (1,121 m) on the prefectural border. Although there are few roads, foot-paths, or man made structures within the site, approximately 3,000 people a year climb Mount Futasumori (1,086m), on the border of the core zone. Siebold's beech (Buna, Fagus crenata) is typical in this Japanese temperate forest, and many hikers visit here for the beautiful autumnal yellow leaves. For more information, visit: http://www.wcmc.org.uk/protected_areas/data/wh/shira.html  (in English), or http://yamagara77.web.infoseek.co.jp/touhoku/shirakami_0105.htm  (in Japanese with photos).
